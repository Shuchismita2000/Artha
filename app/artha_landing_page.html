The problem

Two ways a "qualified lead" quietly fails

Traditional lead scoring optimizes for a single signal. That creates two failure modes that explain low conversion — both get counted as "leads" today.

Failure mode 1

High intent, low affordability

Browses loan pages, uses the EMI calculator repeatedly — but actual repayment capacity doesn't support the loan size the RM ends up pitching. Interest, not disbursal.

Failure mode 2

High affordability, low intent

Looks creditworthy on paper — stable salary, clean bureau score — but has shown no behavioral signal of wanting to borrow right now. Qualified, but cold outreach.

Artha scores both independently — from transaction and behavior data respectively — then ranks only where the two genuinely intersect.
How it works

Four stages, no LLM calls in the core pipeline

Deliberately: rules-based parsing and gradient-boosted trees are cheaper at scale and more defensible to a bank's compliance function than a black-box model's reasoning.

01

Data layer

Customer master, 18 months of transactions, 6 months of app/behavioral logs.

02

Processing

Rules-based narration parser categorizes every transaction. No API calls.

03

ML layer

XGBoost income model + LightGBM intent model, each with SHAP explainability.

04

Lead score

Intent × affordability × product-fit — ranked, explainable, backtested.

Income verification

Self-declared income isn't always the real number

The income model is trained only on salaried customers, where declared income is payslip-verifiable — then applied to self-employed and business-owner customers, whose declared income is systematically noisier.

Median declared income ₹38,417/mo
Artha estimated income AI-estimated ₹47,912/mo
Median gap +17.8%

Median figures across 2,010 self-employed and business-owner customers in our backtest.

52.8% of that segment understated income by more than 15% — exactly the gap prudent underwriting needs to catch before it affects loan sizing.
